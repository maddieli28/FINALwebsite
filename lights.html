Neon signage in Hong Kong has long been emblematic of the city's dynamic and visually arresting nighttime landscape. During the latter half of the 20th century, these glowing emblems became synonymous with the city's rapid economic growth and cosmopolitan allure, bathing its bustling streets in colorful, luminescent hues. From bustling Nathan Road to the quaint alleyways of Central, neon signs advertising everything from tiny noodle shops to grand cinemas created a vivid tapestry that defined Hong Kong's urban aesthetic.

However, the tide began to turn for neon signage with advances in technology and shifting regulatory landscapes. The rise of LED technology brought about a new era of street signage that was cheaper, more energy-efficient, and required less maintenance than traditional neon. Consequently, many of the city's iconic neon signs have been dismantled and replaced, leading to a noticeable change in the visual character of Hong Kong's nightscape.

Despite this, there is a strong movement to preserve the cultural heritage associated with neon signage. Enthusiasts and cultural historians see these glowing artifacts as more than mere advertisements; they represent a significant era in Hong Kong's history, characterized by both creativity and cosmopolitanism. Efforts to save and celebrate neon include museum exhibits, art installations, and the documentation of the craft involved in making these signs. Today, neon signs continue to inspire artists and filmmakers, evoking nostalgia and serving as a bridge to the past.
