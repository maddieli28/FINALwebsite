British Influence and Western Traditions: The longstanding British influence, evident in Hong Kong's legal system, education, and cultural festivals, may see a decline. Events with colonial origins or Western styles, such as the Hong Kong Sevens rugby tournament or various art festivals that celebrate Western forms of art, might receive less emphasis or undergo changes to incorporate more Chinese elements. The celebration of Christmas and Easter, while not directly tied to British governance, also reflects Western cultural penetration that might be subtly de-emphasized in public life.

Local Festivals and Holidays: Currently, Hong Kong has a vibrant mix of Eastern and Western celebrations, including Chinese New Year, the Mid-Autumn Festival, and the Dragon Boat Festival, alongside more localized events like the Cheung Chau Bun Festival and the Tai Hang Fire Dragon Dance. These locally significant festivals, deeply rooted in Hong Kong's own stories and traditions, could face changes in how they are celebrated, with potentially more influence from the mainland's interpretations or methods of celebration. The aim might be to foster a stronger Pan-Chinese identity, aligning Hong Kong's public cultural expressions more closely with those seen across other cities in China.

Cultural Heritage Initiatives: Efforts to preserve Hong Kong's unique cultural heritage, such as the conservation of historical sites, promotion of Cantonese opera, and preservation of the Cantonese language in public life, might be impacted. There could be a strategic shift towards initiatives that align more with national narratives or priorities, such as promoting Mandarin language usage and highlighting historical narratives that are centered around Chinese identity and heritage, potentially at the expense of projects that focus exclusively on Hong Kong's colonial past and its unique local culture.
