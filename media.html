In contrast to the rest of China, where control over media is pervasive, Hong Kong's freedom of speech, of the press, and of publication are protected under Article 27 of the Hong Kong Basic Law and Article 16 of the Hong Kong Bill of Rights.

Post-2047, as Hong Kong transitions fully under Chinese control, there could be significant implications for media freedom and freedom of speech. Historically, Hong Kong has enjoyed a high degree of press freedom compared to mainland China, where the media is heavily regulated by the state. However, with the end of the "One Country, Two Systems" framework, there is a potential for increased restrictions to be implemented in Hong Kong. These could manifest as greater censorship of newspapers, television, and digital content, aligning more closely with the stringent controls observed in the mainland. Additionally, laws that govern speech, particularly regarding criticism of the government and its policies, may be tightened. This shift could lead to a reduction in the open, critical discourse that has been a feature of Hong Kong's media, impacting journalists, content creators, and the general public. Such changes would not only alter the media landscape but could also affect the city's international reputation as a place of relatively free expression.
