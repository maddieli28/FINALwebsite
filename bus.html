Hong Kong's double-decker buses have been an iconic part of the city's public transport system since the mid-20th century, with their towering frames and vibrant colors becoming a signature of the urban landscape. These buses, operated by companies like Kowloon Motor Bus (KMB) and Citybus, have transitioned from older diesel models to modern, low-floor and electric designs to align with sustainability goals. A notable event in this history occurred in 2024, when enthusiasts and residents gathered to bid farewell to Hong Kong’s last non-low-floor bus, the Volvo Olympian, marking the end of an era. The final journey turned into a city-wide celebration, with passengers reminiscing and honoring the legacy of a vehicle that once symbolized the evolution of Hong Kong's public transportation.

Hong Kong is actively transitioning to a zero-emission public transportation system, aiming for carbon neutrality by 2050. The government has set a target to introduce approximately 700 electric buses by the end of 2027.

The Kowloon Motor Bus Company (KMB) plans to upgrade its entire fleet to new energy buses by 2040. In line with this initiative, Alexander Dennis is introducing battery-electric vehicles to the city, including the Enviro100EV—a compact single-decker bus designed to seat up to 26 passengers and accommodate up to 45 passengers in total.
